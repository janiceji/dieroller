Janice Ji

Die Rolling

The package "dieroller" is a minimal implementation for simulating rolling a die a given number of times, and to visualize the relative frequencies in such a series of rolls

Creating a die

The first step is to create a "die" object with the function die():

mydie <- die()
mydie
#> object "die"
#> 
#>   side      prob
#> 1    1 0.1666667
#> 2    2 0.1666667
#> 3    3 0.1666667
#> 4    4 0.1666667
#> 5    5 0.1666667
#> 6    6 0.1666667

By default, die() creates a fair die with standard sides 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6, and with probability values for each side of 1/6.

You can change the defaults with arguments sides and prob:

loaded_die <- die(sides = c('a', 'b', 'c', 'd', 'e', 'f'), prob = c(0.3, 0.4, 0.1, 0.1, 0.1, 0))
loaded_die
#> object "die"
#> 
#>   side prob
#> 1    a  0.3
#> 2    b  0.4
#> 3    c  0.1
#> 4    d  0.1
#> 5    e  0.1
#> 6    f  0.0

Rolling a die

Once you have defined a "die" object, you can use the function roll() to roll a die a given number of times. The output will be an object of class "roll", which will contain the vector of rolls:

roll100 <- roll(mydie, times = 100)
roll100
#> object "roll"
#> 
#> $rolls
#>   [1] 4 1 6 1 1 4 2 4 3 6 1 1 1 3 3 4 6 3 3 5 2 5 5 4 5 1 4 6 4 1 2 6 5 4 2
#>  [36] 3 1 4 6 1 3 1 4 6 3 3 5 3 4 5 5 1 5 5 3 1 2 6 4 2 3 3 1 2 6 1 2 1 5 5
#>  [71] 5 2 6 1 3 3 6 5 3 3 1 3 6 4 3 1 4 5 4 1 4 4 4 2 5 3 3 3 2 6

The summary() function gives you a nice table of frequencies:

summary(roll100)
#> summary "roll"
#> 
#>   side count prop
#> 1    1    20 0.20
#> 2    2    11 0.11
#> 3    3    22 0.22
#> 4    4    18 0.18
#> 5    5    16 0.16
#> 6    6    13 0.13

Plot of relative frequencies

You can use the function plot() to create a bar graph with the relative frequencies of of die rolls:

plot(roll100)
